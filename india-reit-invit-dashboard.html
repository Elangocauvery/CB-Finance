📖 What is a REIT? (Real Estate Investment Trust)
Definition: A REIT (Real Estate Investment Trust) is a SEBI-regulated trust that owns and operates income-generating real estate — primarily Grade-A commercial offices, retail malls, and hospitality. By law, REITs must distribute at least 90% of net distributable cash flows (NDCF) quarterly to unitholders.

Business model — how REITs make money:
① Collect rent from blue-chip tenants (MNCs, GCCs, domestic corporates) under long-term leases (5–10 years)
② Built-in rent escalations of ~15% every 3 years lock in growing income
③ Re-lease expiring space at higher market rents (re-leasing spreads of 17–27% currently)
④ Acquire more assets using unit capital + debt → grow the NOI base
⑤ Distribute 90%+ of cash flows as quarterly DPU (Distribution Per Unit)

Structure: Sponsor → sets up the REIT trust → appoints an Investment Manager and Trustee → REIT holds assets directly or through SPVs (Special Purpose Vehicles) → unitholders receive distributions

Key metrics for REIT analysis: NOI (Net Operating Income), DPU (Distribution Per Unit), Distribution Yield (DPU ÷ Unit Price), NAV (Net Asset Value), Occupancy %, WALE (Weighted Avg. Lease Expiry), Re-leasing spreads, LTV (Loan-to-Value), Cost of Debt
🏢 Office REITs (Embassy, Mindspace, Brookfield, KRT)
Own Grade-A office parks leased to MNCs, GCCs, Fortune 500 companies. Long lease terms (5–10 yrs) with 3-yearly escalations. Demand driven by India's GCC boom — companies like JP Morgan, Microsoft, Google, Amazon prefer institutional Grade-A office space. NOI margins typically 70–90%. WALE 5–8 years.
🛍 Retail REITs (Nexus Select Trust)
Own premium shopping malls leased to national and international retail brands. Revenue = base rent + variable rent linked to tenant turnover. Mall occupancy at 95%+. WALE shorter (3–4 yrs, retail norm). Re-classified by SEBI as Equity REIT (Jan 1, 2026). Consumption-driven — benefits from India's premiumisation wave.
📋 REIT vs FD/Bond — why investors choose it
• Yield 6–8.5% (vs FD 6.5–7%) + capital appreciation potential
• Tax-efficient distributions (mix of dividend, interest, capital repayment)
• Liquidity — tradeable on NSE/BSE like stocks
• Inflation hedge — built-in rent escalations
• SEBI-regulated, quarterly distributions mandatory
• No direct property ownership hassles
• Minimum investment: just 1 unit (₹118–₹422)
🏗 What is an InvIT? (Infrastructure Investment Trust)
Definition: An InvIT (Infrastructure Investment Trust) is a SEBI-regulated trust that owns and operates infrastructure assets — highways, toll roads, power transmission lines, gas pipelines, warehousing/logistics parks, and telecom towers. Like REITs, InvITs must distribute 90% of NDCF to unitholders.

Business model — how InvITs make money:
① Toll roads: Collect vehicle tolls daily under NHAI/government concession agreements (20–30 years). Revenue grows with traffic volume + annual toll price hikes
② Power transmission: Collect fixed "transmission tariffs" from state power utilities. Revenue is government-backed and extremely stable (near-annuity income)
③ Warehousing/Logistics: Collect rent from e-commerce, FMCG, pharma, auto tenants on long leases
④ Gas pipelines: Charge usage fees for gas transportation — regulated tariff-based model
⑤ Acquire more assets using unit capital + debt → grow NDCF → grow DPU

Key metrics for InvIT analysis: NDCF (Net Distributable Cash Flow), DPU, Distribution Yield, EBITDA, Concession period remaining, Traffic volume growth (for toll), Availability (for transmission), LTV, Cost of Debt, Asset life/WAL (Weighted Asset Life)
🛣 Toll Road InvITs (IRB InvIT, NHIT, Cube, Highways)
Collect toll revenue under NHAI concessions (20–30 yrs). Revenue = traffic × toll rate. Annual toll hikes (inflation-linked, ~5–8% p.a.) built in. Risk: traffic volume dependent on economic activity. NHAI-sponsored NHIT is quasi-sovereign backed. Longest concession period = highest asset life.
⚡ Power Transmission InvITs (IndiGrid, PowerGrid)
Collect fixed "availability charges" from state grid operators — paid regardless of actual power flow. Effectively annuity income. Backed by CERC (Central Electricity Regulatory Commission) approvals. IndiGrid sponsored by KKR + Sterlite Power. PowerGrid InvIT backed by GoI's PGCIL — sovereign quasi-backing. Extremely stable, low-risk cash flows.
📦 Logistics InvITs (TVS Infrastructure Trust)
Own warehousing and industrial parks leased to e-commerce, automotive, FMCG, pharma, electronics companies. Revenue = rent + CAM charges. Backed by IFC (World Bank) and L&T. TVS ILP part of TVS Mobility Group (Chennai). Growing rapidly with India's logistics formalisation trend and GST-driven warehouse consolidation.
📡 Telecom Tower InvITs (Altius Telecom)
Passive telecom infrastructure — towers leased to mobile operators (Jio, Airtel, Vi). Revenue = tower rental income under long-term Master Service Agreements. Beneficiary of 5G rollout and rising tenancy ratios. Altius is the listed example — sector poised for more InvIT listings as tower companies monetise assets.

⚖ SEBI Regulations — REITs & InvITs: Complete Framework
SEBI regulates both REITs and InvITs under the SEBI (Real Estate Investment Trusts) Regulations, 2014 and SEBI (Infrastructure Investment Trusts) Regulations, 2014 respectively — with ongoing amendments to align with equity market norms. The most recent significant updates were the April 2025 Amendment Regulations and the March 28, 2025 Master Circular amendments covering sponsor lock-in, follow-on offers, and unit-based employee benefit schemes.
1. Sponsor — Who Qualifies & Obligations
Who is a Sponsor? The Sponsor is the entity that sets up (settles) the REIT or InvIT trust and transfers assets into it. The sponsor is typically the real estate developer or infrastructure company that originally built or owns the underlying assets. Examples: Embassy Group + Blackstone (Embassy REIT), NHAI (NHIT InvIT), IRB Infrastructure (IRB InvIT), Sattva Group + Blackstone (KRT), KKR + Sterlite Power (IndiGrid).
Minimum Unitholding — REITs (SEBI Reg. 11): Sponsor(s) and their group entities must collectively hold a minimum of 15% of the outstanding units of the REIT at all times. Perpetual requirement
Minimum Unitholding — InvITs (SEBI Reg. 12): Sponsor(s) and their group entities must collectively hold a minimum of 15% of outstanding units at all times. If the project manager is not a sponsor or sponsor associate → minimum raised to 25%. Perpetual requirement
2. Lock-in Rules (as amended March 2025)
Pre-2025 rule: Sponsors had to lock in 25% of total unit capital for 3 years from trading approval; all units above 25% locked in for 1 year.

Current rule (post March 28, 2025 amendment):
• 15% of units allotted to sponsor & sponsor group locked in for 3 years from date of trading approval (condition: project manager must be sponsor or associate)
• Remaining units locked in for 1 year from trading approval
• If project manager is NOT sponsor/associate: 25% locked in for 3 years (higher standard)

Note: This is a significant easing — the lock-in now applies to units allotted to the sponsor, not to 25% of total unit capital. This gives sponsors more flexibility to raise capital via new unit issuances.
Inter-se transfers within Sponsor Group (NEW — 2025): Locked-in units may now be transferred among sponsor and sponsor group entities. Lock-in continues with the transferee for the remaining original period. Transferee cannot further sell until lock-in expires. In multi-sponsor trusts, transfer only within same sponsor group — cannot cross to another sponsor's group.
Change of Sponsor: When a sponsor exits and a new sponsor comes in, locked-in units from the outgoing sponsor may be transferred to the incoming sponsor (or self-sponsored investment manager), subject to minimum unitholding requirements being met post-transfer.
3. Investment Conditions (Asset Allocation)
80:20 Rule (REITs & InvITs): Minimum 80% of assets must be in completed, revenue-generating properties/projects at all times. Maximum 20% may be in under-construction assets, listed/unlisted debt securities, or other permissible investments.
HoldCo cash flow rule (2025 clarification): Holding companies (HoldCos) may now offset their own negative cash flows against inflows from SPVs before distributing upward to the REIT/InvIT — provided adequate disclosures are made. Previously, holdcos had to distribute 100% of cash received from SPVs regardless of their own balance sheets.
4. Distribution Rules
Mandatory distribution — REITs: Must distribute ≥90% of net distributable cash flows (NDCF) to unitholders. Distributions must be made at least once every quarter. Components: (a) dividend from SPVs, (b) interest on inter-company loans, (c) repayment of SPV debt. Tax treatment of each component differs in the hands of unitholders.
Mandatory distribution — InvITs: Must distribute ≥90% of NDCF at least once every 6 months (most do quarterly in practice). Similar component structure to REITs.
Public unitholding: Minimum 25% of units must be held by the public at all times. Units held by related parties of the sponsor, investment manager, or project manager do NOT count as public — unless those entities are Qualified Institutional Buyers (QIBs).
5. Leverage & Debt Rules
Leverage cap — REITs: Debt-to-total-assets ratio cannot exceed 49%. Unit holder approval required if debt exceeds 25% of asset value (25–49% band requires unitholder approval at each additional borrowing). Credit rating mandatory when total borrowings exceed specified thresholds.
Leverage cap — InvITs: Debt cannot exceed 49% of value of InvIT assets. Same unitholder approval thresholds as REITs for the 25–49% band. The 80% completed asset rule also constrains leverage indirectly.
Credit rating (2025 amendment): SEBI clarified that the "issuer credit rating of the REIT/InvIT" must be obtained (not just project-level ratings) when borrowings cross threshold levels. This enhances transparency for unitholders.
6. Governance & Trustee Rules (2025 Amendments)
Trustee obligations (new 2025 framework): Trustee must now: (a) comply with core principles of transparency, accountability, due diligence; (b) act impartially in fiduciary capacity; (c) prioritise unitholder protection; (d) maintain effective oversight over the Investment Manager; (e) submit quarterly confirmations that trustee has not engaged in unit transactions of the trust it manages. Net worth compliance certificates required from IM on half-yearly basis and from sponsor + IM annually.
NRC composition: Nomination & Remuneration Committee (NRC) of the Investment Manager can now comprise non-executive directors — provided at least 2/3 are independent directors.
REIT equity reclassification (Jan 1, 2026): SEBI reclassified all REITs from "Hybrid" to "Equity" instruments effective January 1, 2026. This broadens eligibility for insurance, pension fund, and sovereign wealth fund mandates that have minimum equity allocation requirements. Nexus Select Trust was particularly impacted positively as it was previously Hybrid.
7. SM REIT Rules (Small & Medium REITs)
Asset size: SM REITs cover assets valued ₹50 crore – ₹500 crore (assets below this cannot list; above this must use mainboard REIT route).
Minimum investment: ₹10 lakh per unit (HNI/institutional — not retail).
Sponsor holding: Sponsor must hold minimum 5% of units for minimum 2 years post-listing.
Structure: Each scheme under the SM REIT trust is separately listed — each scheme owns a specific property or group of properties.
Distribution: 95% (vs 90% for mainboard REITs) of NDCF to be distributed quarterly.
India's first: PropShare Platina listed on BSE, December 2024 (Property Share Investment Trust).
Key risk for investors re: sponsors: If a sponsor reduces its holding below the minimum 15%, SEBI can take action including directing restoration of holding, restricting further unit sales, or imposing penalties. The perpetual minimum holding requirement is designed to ensure sponsors have "skin in the game" and do not dump assets into the trust and exit. Suryoday SFB's analogous issue — pledged promoter shares 22.35% — illustrates the risk when sponsors/promoters approach their minimum freely-held threshold.
