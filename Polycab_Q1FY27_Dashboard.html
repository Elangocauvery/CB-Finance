THE ONE-PARAGRAPH THESIS
CB READ
Q1 FY27 was a raw-material stress test — and Polycab passed it. Copper and aluminium whipsawed violently during the quarter (Strait of Hormuz / US–Iran escalation drove an April spike; June saw aluminium plunge 18–20% point-to-point and copper correct sharply). A pass-through business should bleed margin in that environment. Instead, EBITDA margin expanded 70 bps QoQ to 13.8%, W&C EBIT hit 13.3%, and PAT set an all-time quarterly record of ₹797 Cr. The mitigation machinery — cost-plus pricing, backward integration, LC-financed procurement, mix management — worked. The flip side: 39% revenue growth flatters; underlying volume grew only low-to-mid single digits on a 26% base, June destocking hit channel offtake as dealers anticipated price cuts, and Polycab cut prices 3–4% in early July. Q2 realizations will be softer. The demand story (T&D, solar, data centres) is intact; the near-term optics will be noisier.

THE Q1 FY27 COMMODITY TIMELINE — WHAT ACTUALLY HAPPENED (APR–JUL 2026)
APR 2026
The escalation spike. Strait of Hormuz disruption and the US–Iran conflict pushed oil to its peak; energy-driven inflation cascaded into metals. Copper and aluminium ran up hard. For a cable maker this means: input cost per km of cable surges, but revenue per km surges too (cost-plus pricing). Channel dealers, seeing prices rising, stocked aggressively — rising-price quarters normally pull demand forward.
APR–MAY 2026
Elevated plateau. Polycab's realizations stayed "commodity-linked favourable" — management explicitly credited favourable commodity-linked realizations for part of the 43% domestic W&C value growth. Volume grew only low-to-mid single digits (on a brutal 26% volume base from Q1 FY26). Roughly speaking, of the 39% consolidated value growth, the majority was price (metal inflation) and mix, not tonnage.
JUN 2026
The crash. Point-to-point (1 Jun → 30 Jun), aluminium plunged 18–20%. Copper, which management said had run to roughly ~14,000 levels, corrected to ~13,100–13,200. The channel flipped instantly from stocking to destocking — dealers stopped buying, anticipating price cuts. Management confirmed quarter-end stocking was "below expectation." This is the classic pass-through paradox: falling input prices are a near-term revenue headwind.
EARLY JUL 2026
The reset. Polycab took a 3–4% price revision (cut) in the first fortnight of July, passing the metal deflation to the market. This restarts channel buying (dealers restock once they believe prices have bottomed) but compresses Q2 FY27 revenue realization per unit. PVC resin simultaneously softened through late June–early July as monsoon slowed construction offtake — a mild tailwind for insulation and FMEG conduit costs.

HOW EACH INPUT HIT THE P&L — AND HOW POLYCAB NEUTRALISED IT
COPPER
Spike → Correction
P&L exposure severityVery High
Mitigation effectiveness in Q1High
Impact: Total expenses +39.9% YoY (₹7,256 Cr) — copper is the biggest reason. Revenue inflated in tandem (+39%), masking single-digit volumes. June's sharp correction triggered channel destocking, denting quarter-end offtake.

Mitigation: (1) Cost-plus pricing — hikes passed within weeks during the run-up; (2) In-house copper rod drawing (backward integration) captures conversion margin and smooths spot exposure; (3) LC-based procurement stretched payables (~80–90 days vs inventory ~100–110 days), letting suppliers' credit finance the costlier metal; (4) order-backed purchasing aligns metal buys to confirmed institutional orders.
ALUMINIUM
−18–20% in June
P&L exposure severityHigh
Mitigation effectiveness in Q1High
Impact: The single most violent move of the quarter — an 18–20% point-to-point collapse within June. Aluminium-heavy products (LT power cables, ABC conductors) faced inventory mark-down risk: metal bought in May was suddenly worth far less as finished-goods pricing reset.

Mitigation: (1) The July 3–4% price cut was calibrated — smaller than the metal fall because copper fell less than aluminium and blended RM cost matters; (2) high inventory turns in cables limit the stale-inventory window; (3) institutional/EPC contracts typically carry price-variation clauses tied to metal indices, transferring the deflation symmetrically; (4) FIFO consumption of pre-crash inventory was cushioned by the wires-heavy, channel-heavy mix which carries better gross margins.
PVC RESIN
Quiet Tailwind
P&L exposure severityModerate
Mitigation effectivenessStructural
Impact: PVC is the insulation/sheathing workhorse and feeds FMEG pipes & conduits (a top-3 FMEG category this quarter). India remains structurally import-dependent (domestic capacity ~1.8 mtpa vs ~4.7 mtpa demand), so anti-dumping duties on suspension resin have narrowed the import-domestic gap and firmed the floor price. Through late June–early July 2026, resin prices softened as monsoon slowed construction — a modest cost tailwind entering Q2.

Mitigation: (1) In-house PVC/FR/FRLS/XLPE compounding — Polycab manufactures its own compounds rather than buying finished compound, capturing spread and controlling quality; (2) diversified resin sourcing across domestic and import channels; (3) FMEG conduit pricing adjusts with resin, same pass-through logic as metals.

✅ What Worked — The Proof Points
🎯Margins expanded into the storm: EBITDA 13.8% (+70 bps QoQ), W&C EBIT 13.3% — at the top of the 11–13% Project Spring guidance band — during the most volatile metal quarter in recent memory.
💰Record PAT ₹797 Cr: input inflation of ~40% on the expense line was fully monetized on the revenue line. The pass-through engine has near-zero leakage.
🏦Balance sheet got stronger, not weaker: net cash ₹3,990 Cr and WC at 15 days even while carrying costlier inventory — the LC strategy did exactly what it was designed to do.
🧵Fibre lock-in: the BharatNet fibre pre-buy is a textbook procurement hedge — high-single-digit EPC margins protected for 2–3 years regardless of fibre spot prices.
⚠️ What To Watch — The Honest Caveats
📉Q2 realization headwind: the 3–4% July price cut plus lower metal prices mechanically deflate revenue per km. Value growth will look weaker even if volumes recover.
📦Destocking overhang: channel stocking was "below expectation" in June. If dealers wait for further cuts, Q2 volume could stay soft before the restock kicks in.
🔄WC normalization: the 15-day working capital is temporary (LC payables). Reversion to 45–50 days will consume cash flow in coming quarters — don't extrapolate Q1 FCF.
🧪Volume vs value honesty: low-to-mid single-digit volume growth is the real demand signal. The 39% headline is a commodity artifact. Watch H2 volume prints on softer bases.
🛢️PVC duty floor: anti-dumping duties protect Indian resin makers but set a firmer cost floor for converters like Polycab if global resin stays weak.

RISKS — RANKED
🔴 Raw Material & Macro Risks
🪙Commodity whiplash (recurring): A second spike-crash cycle in copper/aluminium repeats the Q1 pattern — inflated revenue, destocking air-pockets, price-revision churn. Pass-through protects margin %, not volume visibility.
⚓Strait of Hormuz / US–Iran: Tensions resurfaced in the week before results. Direct hit to Middle East exports (was 20–24% of export mix via Oman/Saudi/UAE) and indirect hit via oil-led metal inflation.
🛢️PVC duty regime: Anti-dumping duties on resin imports firm the cost floor for a structurally import-dependent input (India: ~1.8 mtpa capacity vs ~4.7 mtpa demand) — a slow cost creep until domestic capacity (RIL ~1.5 mtpa by 2027) lands.
📈Rate environment: RBI on hold at 5.25% but flagging INR volatility; a hawkish global turn slows real estate — the bedrock of retail wire demand.
🏢 Company-Specific Risks
🔵Volume optics: Two more quarters of single-digit volume (even on high bases) would test the premium multiple regardless of value growth.
🟠Capex execution: ₹6,000–8,000 Cr over 5 years under Project Spring — return discipline must hold as capacity lands into a more competitive market.
🟣FMEG margin sustainability: Q1's 8% EBIT was seasonality-aided (extended summer). Structural target remains 8–10% by FY30 — expect quarterly wobble.
🟢Rising competition: New entrants attracted by the sector's visible demand; pricing discipline in wires is the industry-wide watch item.
🔷WC reversion: 15 days → 45–50 days normalization will absorb operating cash flow across FY27 — model it, don't be surprised by it.

Aluminium crashed 18–20% inside June and copper corrected sharply, yet EBITDA margin EXPANDED 70 bps QoQ to 13.8%. Mechanically, how did Polycab pull that off?
June-ல் aluminium 18–20% சரிந்தது, copper-ும் correct ஆனது. ஆனால் EBITDA margin 70 bps உயர்ந்து 13.8% ஆனது. இது எப்படி சாத்தியம்?
▼
English
Four gears turned together, per management's own framework on the concall. (1) MIX — WIRES OVER CABLES: wires are the higher-margin, retail-channel, copper-intensive product; they grew high-single-digit in volume while cables grew low-to-mid. A richer wires mix mechanically lifts blended margin. (2) MIX — CHANNEL OVER INSTITUTIONAL: channel/dealer sales outpaced institutional; dealer sales carry better pricing than tender-driven institutional business. (3) TIMING OF THE PASS-THROUGH: price hikes during the April–May run-up were in force through most of the quarter, while the June metal crash hit COSTS faster than PRICES — the 3–4% price cut only came in July. For a few weeks, Polycab sold at spike-era prices while newer metal purchases got cheaper. That's a transient positive spread — the mirror image of the squeeze that pass-through businesses suffer when prices rise faster than they can reprice. (4) OPERATING LEVERAGE: 39% revenue growth over a largely fixed overhead base. The caveat: gears (3) reverses in Q2 — the July price cut applies immediately while some higher-cost inventory (100–110 days of it) still flows through COGS. Expect Q2 margin to give back some of the 70 bps. The structural anchor remains the 11–13% Project Spring band, and 13.3% W&C EBIT sits at its ceiling.

▼
English
It is smart treasury with a transparency bonus — management flagged it unprompted and told investors NOT to extrapolate it. The mechanics: Polycab runs high inventory (~100–110 days — deliberate, to guarantee availability, a competitive weapon in the dealer channel) and low receivables (20–30 days, because 90% of sales flow through channel-financed dealers). Normally payables partially offset inventory, netting to 45–50 days. In Q1, with metal prices spiking, Polycab used letters of credit for raw material purchases — LC terms push payables out to ~80–90 days. Net effect: suppliers' banks financed the expensive copper sitting in Polycab's warehouses, and reported working capital compressed to 15 days. Why it's smart: during a price spike, the cash cost of carrying inventory balloons; LCs transfer that carry to the cheapest available credit. Why it's not a red flag: (a) disclosed proactively with reversion guidance; (b) acceptances are trade finance against real goods in transit, not disguised borrowing for operations; (c) net cash of ₹3,990 Cr means there is no solvency angle whatsoever. The one thing to genuinely check in the annual report: whether LC/acceptance interest is booked in finance costs or netted into COGS — an analyst on the call asked exactly this, and it affects EBITDA comparability by a small amount.

PVC resin gets far less attention than copper. What is Polycab's actual PVC exposure, and how do India's anti-dumping duties and import dependence change the calculus?

▼
English
PVC touches Polycab in two places. FIRST, insulation and sheathing: virtually every building wire and LT cable is sheathed in PVC or specialty compounds (FR, FRLS, HR). Polycab's mitigation here is structural — it manufactures its own PVC and XLPE compounds in-house rather than buying finished compound, which captures the compounding spread and de-risks quality. SECOND, FMEG pipes and conduits — this quarter the third-largest FMEG category by revenue — where resin IS the product cost. The macro setup: India is structurally short PVC (~1.8 mtpa domestic capacity vs ~4.7 mtpa demand), forcing import reliance. Anti-dumping duties on suspension resin from China, Korea, Japan and others narrow the imported-vs-domestic price gap — good for resin makers, but a firmer cost FLOOR for converters like Polycab even when global resin is weak. The current picture is benign: resin softened through late June–early July 2026 as monsoon slowed construction offtake — a modest Q2 cost tailwind for both sheathing and conduits. The medium-term wildcard is supply-side: Reliance's ~1.5 mtpa PVC/CPVC capacity slated around 2027 would structurally deflate Indian resin economics and reduce import exposure. Net: PVC is a manageable, partially self-hedged ~10–15% of the RM basket — it will never break a quarter the way copper can, but it quietly shapes FMEG conduit margins.

English
This is the cleanest single illustration of Polycab's raw-material philosophy in the whole quarter.
