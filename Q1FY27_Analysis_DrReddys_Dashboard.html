🔻 Dr Reddy's Q1 FY27 — a poor quarter. The five reasons, in order of impact.

  1. gRevlimid (lenalidomide) cliff deepened. North America revenue fell ~35% YoY to ₹2,205 Cr as competitive intensity on lenalidomide stepped up further and the volume-tranche advantage Dr Reddy's enjoyed through FY26 shrank. This single molecule was the swing factor behind both the revenue miss and the margin collapse — lenalidomide has historically been one of DRL's highest-margin US products, so losing volume/price on it disproportionately hits EBITDA, not just revenue.
  2. One-time ₹239.7 Cr semaglutide API provision. A charge tied to semaglutide (GLP-1) API inventory and related costs alone shaved roughly 3 percentage points off gross, EBITDA and PBT margins in the quarter. Excluding this item, EBITDA margin would have been closer to ~15.4% — still weak on a YoY basis, but meaningfully better than the reported 12.5%, which somewhat overstates the underlying deterioration.
  3. Gross margin compression. Gross margin fell sharply from 56.9% to 46.5% YoY. This reflects a North America product-mix shift away from high-margin, limited-competition launches (like lenalidomide was) toward base-business generics facing multiple ANDA holders and therefore much thinner pricing.
  4. Base-business US generic price erosion. Beyond lenalidomide specifically, ordinary US generics price erosion continued across the rest of the North America portfolio — a structural, sector-wide headwind (see the US Tariff — Sector Impact tab) that hits Dr Reddy's harder than most domestic-focused peers because North America is still ~27-28% of group revenue, one of the higher NA weightings in the sector.
  5. Unfavourable operating leverage. Total expenses rose 13.6% YoY even as revenue fell, so SG&A and R&D spend — funding new launches, the biosimilars pipeline, the Nestlé/Celevida GLP+ partnership, and the XACDURO in-licensing deal — landed on a smaller revenue base than planned, further compressing margins below what revenue alone would suggest.

🏰 What Diversification Is (and Isn't) Buying Dr Reddy's
✅ What it buys
Revenue resilience — group revenue fell only 5.5% despite a 35% NA collapse, precisely because three other regions are now large enough to matter. It also reduces single-market regulatory/tariff concentration risk over time.
❌ What it doesn't (yet) buy
Margin resilience — India/EM/Europe growth, while strong, has not been enough to offset the margin mix-shift away from high-margin NA exclusivity products. EBITDA margin nearly halved even though revenue only dipped slightly.
🔭 Opportunity
Biosimilars (Toripalimab, semaglutide GLP-1 franchise) and the Nestlé Celevida partnership are positioned to become the next NA/global margin engine as lenalidomide fades — timing and ramp-up speed are the key variables to track.
⚠️ Threat
The new US generic tariff schedule (see next tab) adds a second structural pressure on the NA segment specifically, on top of ordinary post-exclusivity price erosion.
🇺🇸 Why this belongs in a Dr Reddy's report. On 22 Jul 2026 — the same week as Dr Reddy's results — President Trump announced a phased tariff schedule specifically for imported generic drugs, on top of the 100% tariff already in force on patented/branded pharmaceuticals since 2 Apr 2026. Dr Reddy's derives ~27-28% of revenue from North America, almost entirely generics — so this policy sits directly on top of the same segment that just delivered the weak Q1. 🗓️ Policy Timeline — Two Separate Pharma Tariff Tracks
2 Apr 2026
Section 232 Proclamation — Patented/Branded Drugs. 100% tariff on imported patented pharmaceutical products and select APIs. Generics and biosimilars explicitly exempted "at this time." Effective 31 Jul 2026 for 17 large drugmakers named in Annex III; 29 Sep 2026 for everyone else.
22 Jul 2026
Generic Drug Tariff Schedule announced. All generic drug imports remain at 0% tariff for two years starting 1 Aug 2026, then 100% for one year (from ~Aug 2028), then 200% thereafter (from ~Aug 2029). Framed as a "penalty" for companies that don't build US manufacturing capacity within the two-year window. Branded/patented drug policy is unchanged.
Now → Aug 2028
The window. Two years of zero-tariff generic imports — the period every India-to-US generics exporter, Dr Reddy's included, has to either commit capital to US manufacturing, diversify revenue further away from US generics, or accept margin risk from Aug 2028 onward.
Aug 2028 – Jul 2029
100% tariff on non-onshored generic imports.
From Aug 2029
200% tariff — near-prohibitive for companies still exporting finished generics to the US from India without a domestic manufacturing presence.
⚠️ Section 232 authority allows the rate/timeline to be "expressly reduced, modified, or terminated" — trade lawyers and India's Commerce Ministry are already flagging this as a negotiating lever amid ongoing India-US trade talks, not a locked-in three-year outcome.
💊 Dr Reddy's Specifically — Where It Sits
Dr Reddy's is rated Impact-B on the sector scale below. It has no major dedicated US manufacturing base — supply to North America is predominantly India-manufactured and exported — so its ~27-28%-of-revenue NA segment sits inside the tariff's target zone once the 2028 step-up arrives. The silver lining is that this NA segment is already under pressure from ordinary post-exclusivity price erosion (lenalidomide, base generics), so the incremental tariff risk is layering onto a business Dr Reddy's is already working to de-risk through India/EM/Europe growth and its biosimilars pipeline — rather than hitting a business that was otherwise firing on all cylinders.

🇮🇳 Domestic Players — Largely Untouched
Mankind Pharma, Eris Lifesciences, Torrent Pharma and Alkem Laboratories sell overwhelmingly into the India domestic branded-generics market (IPM) — priced, regulated (NPPA/DPCO) and consumed entirely within India. This policy is a US import-tariff on drugs entering the United States; it has no jurisdiction over what these companies sell to Indian patients through Indian pharmacies. For Mankind (82% India) and Eris (95% India) in particular, the entire tariff schedule — 0% today, 100% in 2028, 200% in 2029 — is close to a non-event. Their earnings drivers stay exactly what they were before 22 Jul 2026: chronic-therapy volume growth, price realisation under DPCO, and branded-portfolio expansion (Mankind's BSV vaccines integration, Eris's Kinedex/Oaknet dermatology and nutraceuticals additions, Torrent's JB Pharma synergy). Relative to Dr Reddy's and the US-export-heavy names, this is where the sector's earnings certainty now sits.
🔬 CDMO / API Players — Largely Untouched
Divi's Labs, Laurus Labs, Neuland, Sai Life Sciences, Anthem Biosciences and Supriya Lifescience — plus the CDMO half of Gland Pharma's revenue — sell contract manufacturing and API services to global innovator pharma companies under long-term Master Supply Agreements, not finished generic dosage forms sold at retail in the US. This is a structurally different commercial relationship from what the tariff targets: a CDMO is paid a manufacturing fee under contract, it does not "import a generic drug for resale" in the way a Natco or Aurobindo does when shipping finished lenalidomide or a branded-generic tablet into the US market. Even where the end customer is a US company, the tariff's stated target — reshoring generic drug production — doesn't naturally capture upstream API/intermediate contract manufacturing the same way. This is the second pocket of the sector, alongside India-domestic, that should trade at a relative premium as the tariff timeline plays out — and it is precisely the CDMO exposure Dr Reddy's itself lacks, which is worth watching as a structural gap in its portfolio versus Divi's/Laurus/Gland.
🥇⚠️ Who Gets Hurt Most vs. Who Is Best Positioned

⚠️ Most exposed (Impact-A)

Aurobindo Pharma, Zydus Lifesciences, Natco Pharma — large finished-dose US generics revenue, little to no US manufacturing footprint, no disclosed on-shoring commitment yet. Dr Reddy's (Impact-B) sits one notch better than this group, but in the same broad "at-risk exporter" bucket.

✅ Best positioned (Impact-D/E)

CDMO/API group: Sai Life, Anthem, Divi's, Laurus, Neuland, Supriya. Domestic group: Mankind, Eris, Torrent, Alkem. Neither group depends on the thing this tariff schedule taxes — finished generic drugs imported into the US.

🏬 The Purchasing-Manager / GPO Problem — Why US Generic Prices Stay High for Consumers Even as Dr Reddy's (and Peers) Get Squeezed
This structural issue predates — and will outlast — the tariff headlines, and it's directly relevant to why Dr Reddy's NA margins are under pressure even independent of lenalidomide's specific dynamics.

How the US generic buy-side is structured: Retail pharmacy purchasing is dominated by a small number of mega buying groups pairing the largest chains/wholesalers — Walmart with McKesson (ClarusONE), Walgreens Boots Alliance with Cencora/AmerisourceBergen, and CVS with Cardinal Health (Red Oak Sourcing) — alongside PBM-owned GPOs: CVS Caremark's Zinc Health Services, Cigna/Express Scripts' Ascent Health Services, and UnitedHealth/OptumRx's Emisar Pharma Services. Three PBMs alone control roughly 75-80% of US covered lives.

Effect on manufacturers like Dr Reddy's: This concentrated buy-side gives purchasing organisations heavy negotiating leverage over the many generic manufacturers competing to supply the same molecule — the well-documented, persistent single-digit-to-double-digit annual price erosion that shows up in Dr Reddy's own base-business commentary this quarter is a direct consequence.

Effect on US consumers: The rebates and fees GPOs/PBMs extract from manufacturers are frequently not passed through transparently to the end payer — patient cost-sharing is often calculated off list price rather than the lower net price actually paid, so list prices stay comparatively sticky even as manufacturer realisations fall. The FTC and HHS have separately investigated both GPOs and PBM-owned GPOs for this opacity.

Net read for Dr Reddy's: the company is being squeezed from the supply side (GPO/PBM buying power) at the same time the new tariff schedule threatens to squeeze it from the trade-policy side from 2028 — a genuine pincer specific to its finished-generic NA business, and a structural reason to weight the diversification into India/EM/Europe/biosimilars as more than just a "nice to have."

🏆 Verdict
⚠️

Q1 FY27: A Concentrated Miss, Not a Broken Business

Dr Reddy's delivered a genuinely poor Q1 FY27 headline — PAT down 69% YoY, EBITDA margin nearly halved — and the market was right to mark the stock down on it. But the underlying story is narrower than the headline suggests: this was overwhelmingly a North America / lenalidomide story plus a one-off provision, landing in a quarter where India, Emerging Markets and Europe all grew strongly. The balance sheet is untouched and solid.

The bigger strategic question this report raises isn't Q1 itself — it's what comes next. Dr Reddy's now carries two overlapping North America risks: (1) ordinary post-exclusivity price erosion on its generics base, which just showed up in this quarter's numbers, and (2) the new US generic tariff schedule from 2028, which will apply to that same NA business unless Dr Reddy's builds US manufacturing capacity or further shifts its revenue mix within the two-year window. Unlike the sector's CDMO players (Divi's, Laurus, Sai Life, Anthem) or domestic-pure-plays (Mankind, Eris, Torrent), Dr Reddy's doesn't have a large structurally-insulated segment to fall back on — its insulation is coming from geographic diversification (India/EM/Europe) and a still-maturing biosimilars/GLP-1 pipeline, not from a different business model.

Bottom line: this is a name for investors willing to underwrite a multi-quarter recovery story on NA stabilisation and biosimilar ramp-up, at a valuation the Street has already reset lower — not a name to chase on the sequential PAT recovery alone. Watch Q2/Q3 FY27 for confirmation that the NA base (ex-lenalidomide) has stopped shrinking before treating this as a clean turnaround.
