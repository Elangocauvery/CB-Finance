Where it stands today (20 Jul 2026): JSW Steel's ₹19,700 Cr resolution plan for Bhushan Power & Steel Ltd (BPSL) is judicially settled in JSW's favour. The Supreme Court's 26 Sep 2025 ruling upheld the plan and reversed its own earlier liquidation order — restoring the "commercial wisdom of the CoC" doctrine that underpins India's IBC framework. BPSL is now itself being deconsolidated into a 50:50 JV with Japan's JFE Steel.

BPSL business was transferred to the JV via slump sale in March 2026, so FY26/FY27 production bases have been retrospectively adjusted for comparability.
Why the Reversal Matters — Systemic Reading
The episode became a stress test for the credibility of India's Insolvency and Bankruptcy Code itself, not just for JSW. Had the May 2 liquidation order stood, it would have been the largest liquidation by debt size under the IBC, forced banks to reverse recoveries and take fresh provisions, and cast doubt on the finality of court-approved resolution plans generally — a risk flagged by the Department of Financial Services as it consulted lenders in early 2026. The September reversal restored the principle that a resolution plan, once approved through the CoC's commercial wisdom and upheld by NCLT/NCLAT, should not be reopened years later absent clear illegality — legal commentators framed it as sharpening the line between insolvency law and later regulatory second-guessing.
Residual Risk for JSW Steel
Not fully closed: some creditor and promoter-side parties may still pursue further remedies, and the amalgamation of BMM Ispat Ltd with JSW Steel — a separate matter — remains subject to regulatory approvals with no financial impact yet recognised. The BPSL matter itself is treated by the company and most brokerages as resolved, but investors should track any residual review/curative petitions given the case's unusual two-reversal history. Dutch prosecutors separately summoned Tata Steel's Ijmuiden unit in Jul 2026 over pollution allegations — unrelated to JSW, but a reminder that legal/regulatory tail-risk in steel is not confined to one company.

Structural Caveat — the "Re-Export" Loophole
Even with BIS-certification requirements structurally limiting direct commercial sale of Chinese HRC into India, the advance-authorisation re-export scheme remains a channel through which Chinese material can still reach the Indian market — a regulatory carve-out that an anti-dumping determination cannot automatically close without separate policy intervention. This is the key reason JSW Steel (jointly with SAIL, RINL and Usha Martin in earlier filings) continues to press for both anti-dumping and safeguard cover simultaneously rather than relying on either alone.
Net Impact for JSW Steel
Layered trade defence (safeguard + anti-dumping, stacked across flat-product categories) supports domestic HRC/CRC price floors precisely as JSW ramps ~12-13 MT of new capacity toward its FY30 targets — reducing the risk that new volumes get absorbed only via price cuts. The June 2026 HRC anti-dumping probe against China, Japan and Russia is the single most consequential pending action to watch, given JSW's scale in flat products; a final affirmative finding (typically 12-18 months after initiation) would be a structural tailwind, while continued delay leaves flat-steel pricing exposed to global oversupply cycles.

POSCO Greenfield JV (Newly Announced)
JSW Steel has announced a joint venture with South Korea's POSCO to set up a greenfield 6 MTPA integrated steel plant at Dhenkanal, Odisha — adding a second Odisha growth vector alongside the wholly-owned Utkal project. Details on capex split, timelines and product focus are still emerging as of Q1 FY27 disclosures; this materially raises JSW's medium-term capacity trajectory beyond the ~50 MTPA India roadmap if it proceeds on schedule.
Capex Discipline & Funding
FY27 consolidated capex guidance stands at ₹22,000-24,000 Cr, against Q1 FY27 spend of ₹4,869 Cr — tracking broadly in line for the year. The BPSL-JFE JV cash inflow (₹15,700 Cr total, second ₹7,875 Cr tranche received by June-end) plus a proposed ₹811 Cr OFS in the JSW One Platforms IPO are explicit balance-sheet-funding levers management is using alongside operating cash flow to fund this multi-year, multi-site expansion without over-leveraging — net debt fell 14.3% QoQ this quarter even as gross capex continued.

How JSW Could Out-Execute Tata Steel From Here — Reader Framework
Geographic Simplicity
Structural
JSW's overseas footprint (Ohio + Piombino) is small relative to consolidated scale and both units are EBITDA-positive/improving this quarter. Tata Steel continues to carry a much larger, structurally challenged European base (UK electric-arc-furnace transition costs, a Dutch pollution-related prosecutorial summons issued 8 Jul 2026 to Tata Steel Ijmuiden). Lever: JSW's cleaner geography means more of every incremental rupee of India volume growth converts to consolidated profit, without European drag diluting it.
Product-Mix Premiumisation
Execution
JSW's ongoing downstream buildout — CRNO electrical steel, new galvanising lines, the JFE electrical-steel JV targeting EV/transformer demand — pushes the portfolio toward higher-value, less commoditised products where Chinese anti-dumping actions (CRNO, HRC probe) provide a firmer pricing floor. Lever: continuing to out-invest peers in value-added downstream capacity converts capacity growth into margin growth, not just volume growth.
Balance-Sheet Optionality
Financial
The BPSL-JFE monetisation (₹15,700 Cr) and the JSW One Platforms OFS (up to ₹811 Cr) show JSW actively using non-core/partial monetisation to fund its ~50 MTPA India roadmap and the new POSCO JV without relying solely on fresh debt. Lever: sustaining this "monetise-and-reinvest" discipline — rather than pure leverage — would let JSW keep expanding capacity while continuing to deleverage, a combination Tata Steel's more debt-laden European legacy makes structurally harder to replicate at the same pace.
