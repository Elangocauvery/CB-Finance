Business Profile · What UltraTech does · Plants · Products · Brand architecture
Company Overview

UltraTech Cement Limited is India's largest cement manufacturer and the world's third-largest outside China. Headquartered in Mumbai, it is the flagship cement business of the Aditya Birla Group. Product range: Ordinary Portland Cement (OPC), Portland Pozzolana Cement (PPC), Portland Slag Cement (PSC), Ready-Mix Concrete (RMC), White Cement, and Dry Mortars — plus a new Cables & Wires business launching Q3 FY27.

Consolidated grey cement capacity stood at 205.5 MTPA as of 30 Jun 2026 (domestic 200.1 MTPA), spanning integrated clinker plants and grinding units pan-India, with a growing footprint in the UAE, Sri Lanka and Bahrain. The company operates under a multi-brand architecture — the flagship UltraTech brand plus recently-converted India Cements and Kesoram regional brands in the South.

The 4 Cost Centres of a Cement Company — How UltraTech Manages Each · ~84% of total Q1 FY27 expenses sit in these four lines
Every integrated cement manufacturer's cost structure is dominated by four line items: (1) Power & Fuel, (2) Freight & Forwarding (logistics), (3) Raw Materials (limestone, gypsum, fly ash/slag), and (4) Packaging & Other Overheads. Together these accounted for roughly ₹17,952 Cr of UltraTech's ₹21,286 Cr total Q1 FY27 consolidated expenses (~84%). The company's Q1 FY27 outperformance versus peers is explained almost entirely by how it manages the first two of these four — scale, fuel-mix flexibility and logistics density are UltraTech's biggest structural edges.

West Asia Crisis — How UltraTech Held Up on Fuel Costs · Sector-wide input shock, company-specific resilience
The shock: Geopolitical tensions in West Asia through H1 CY2026 pushed up prices of petcoke, imported coal, shipping freight and polypropylene packaging resin — all inputs the Indian cement industry sources partly through Middle East-linked supply chains. Brokerage estimates (JM Financial) put the YoY rise in coal and petcoke costs at 26–43% for the sector in Q1 FY27, with average industry EBITDA margin seen contracting ~1.7 percentage points YoY to ~15.7%. Industry executives estimated a combined ₹250–300/tonne cost impact across H1 FY27 from fuel, freight and packaging-bag inflation.

Why UltraTech Outperformed Peers Anyway
  • Scale & sourcing power: as India's largest buyer of kiln fuel, UltraTech has more contract/spot flexibility than mid-sized peers to time purchases and diversify origin countries.
  • Fuel-mix agility: actively rotated toward cheaper domestic coal as petcoke's relative price advantage narrowed — a lever smaller players with less blending flexibility in their kiln design cannot always pull.
  • Realisation growth absorbed cost: grey cement realisation rose 3.7% YoY to ₹5,218/tonne, with the industry taking 2.5–3% price hikes in the quarter, partially offsetting fuel inflation.
  • WHRS & renewables: waste-heat recovery and >1 GW green power reduce grid/thermal power dependence, a structural (not one-quarter) offset to fossil-fuel cost inflation.
  • Volume leverage: 12.2% consolidated volume growth spread fixed costs over a larger base, protecting EBITDA/tonne even as per-unit fuel cost rose.
Outlook — Is the Worst Over?

Industry trackers (Whalesbook, Jul 2026) note petcoke and coal prices have already receded 10–16% from their recent peaks as the acute phase of the West Asia disruption eases, though the benefit will show up with a lag as older, higher-cost fuel inventory is worked through. Management's own Q2 FY27 guidance (₹130–140/tonne further headwind) suggests the pass-through is not yet complete — H1 FY27 will likely remain the toughest cost window, with a margin recovery more visible in H2 FY27 if fuel prices stay lower and the industry sustains recent price hikes.

🎓 Cement School · Everything you need to know about the cement industry, through UltraTech's lens · EIL Research
Why Cement Matters More Than You Think
Cement is not a commodity — it is the backbone of every road, bridge, dam, home, and factory in India. Every ₹1 lakh crore spent on infrastructure requires roughly 0.8–1 MT of cement. India consumes ~420 MT per year, second only to China. As the sector's largest player, UltraTech's cost structure and scale decisions are effectively a proxy for how the whole industry is run — understanding UltraTech is understanding the industry.
Chapter 1 — Clinker: The Heart of Cement
The Most Critical Asset in the Entire Value Chain
What Is Clinker?

Clinker is the intermediate product formed when limestone, clay, iron ore, and silica are blended and burned at 1,450°C in a rotary kiln. It emerges as dark grey pellets — the actual "binding agent" that gives cement its strength.

Think of clinker as baked dough and cement as the finished bread. You bake at one location (integrated plant), transport the dough, and finish the bread near the customer (grinding unit). That logic is exactly how UltraTech's 20+ integrated plants and 27+ grinding units work together.

UltraTech's Clinker Position
Clinker Capacity (est.)~140 MTPA
Integrated Plants20+ across India
Grinding Units27+
Clinker Factor (est.)~65%

Clinker factor = clinker used per tonne of cement produced. Lower is better — more fly ash/slag blending means cheaper cement and better margins. Best-in-class peers run closer to 51–55%; UltraTech's blended-cement push (PPC/PSC growth) is a direct lever on this Raw Materials cost centre.

Chapter 2 — Grinding Units & the Freight Math
Getting Cement to the Customer Cheaply Is Half the Battle
The Freight Math

Cement sells at roughly ₹5,000–5,200/tonne. Road freight costs ₹0.8–1.2 per tonne per km. Moving cement 300 km eats ₹240–360/T — 5–8% of revenue, gone just in transport. This is why cement is almost always sold within 250–350 km of the plant.

DistanceFreight Cost% of Revenue
100 km (local)₹80–120/T~2%
300 km (regional)₹240–360/T~6%
500 km (long haul)₹400–600/T~10%
How UltraTech Wins This Game

27+ grinding units placed near demand centres convert UltraTech into a "local supplier" in almost every major Indian city — a structural freight advantage few peers can match at scale. That density is exactly why UltraTech's average lead distance fell to 360 km (−9 km YoY) in Q1 FY27 even as volumes grew 12%+.

Key Insight: The India Cements and Kesoram acquisitions weren't just about adding capacity — they slot new plants directly into South India's demand geography, shrinking lead distances in a region UltraTech was previously under-weight in.
Chapter 3 — Power & Fuel: The Volatile Giant
Why a Middle East Conflict Can Move a Cement Company's Profit

Coal and petcoke together typically make up 35–40% of a cement company's total cost — the single largest, and most volatile, line item. This is exactly the input the West Asia conflict disrupted in FY27.

The WHRS & Green Power Race

Waste Heat Recovery Systems (WHRS) capture hot gases exiting the kiln and pre-heater and convert them to near-zero-marginal-cost electricity. UltraTech is scaling WHRS toward a 511 MW group target by FY27, alongside >1 GW of renewable power capacity already installed.

Pet Coke vs Coal: Pet coke is usually cheaper but became relatively costlier than coal in Q1 FY27 as West Asia disruption hit petcoke supply specifically — UltraTech's kilns can burn either, letting it swing the mix and blunt the impact. Not every kiln design allows this flexibility.
Chapter 4 — Why Size Matters Enormously
Cement Is a Fixed-Cost Business — Bigger Is Almost Always Better
Why Cement Rewards Scale

A cement plant's biggest costs — kiln depreciation, captive power, mines, WHRS, management — are largely fixed. The more you produce, the lower the cost per tonne. Scale also brings brand pricing power: UltraTech's realisation of ₹5,218/tonne in Q1 FY27 (+3.7% YoY) reflects a premium over many regional brands — pure margin, no extra production cost.

Scale also enables cross-subsidisation — a large player can compete hard in one region while profits from other regions cushion the impact. A small regional player has no such buffer, which is exactly why the West Asia cost shock hit sector peers harder than it hit UltraTech.

The Capacity Land Grab

India's cement demand is projected to reach 600–700 MT by 2030 from ~420 MT today. Whoever builds capacity now, at today's costs, becomes the lowest-cost producer when that demand arrives. UltraTech's ₹17,000 Cr committed capex over the next 2–2.5 years, targeting 212.7 MTPA domestic by end-FY27 and ~242 MTPA consolidated by FY28, is a direct bet on this logic.

Building a new integrated plant today costs ~₹600–800 Cr per MTPA — in 5 years, likely ₹900–1,100 Cr with inflation. The incentive is to build ahead of the curve.
