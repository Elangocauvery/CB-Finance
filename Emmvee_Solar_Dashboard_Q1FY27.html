Part 1 — Solar Business Models in India
🏆 Model 1 — Integrated Cell + Module Manufacturer Emmvee's Model
This is the most complete value chain model in Indian solar manufacturing. The company manufactures solar cells from silicon wafers, then assembles them into modules (panels). Emmvee operates 2.94 GW of cell manufacturing capacity (TOPCon technology) and 10.3 GW of module capacity.

Why it matters: ALMM List-II came into force on June 1, 2026 — only integrated manufacturers with approved cell capacity can sell modules to government-funded solar projects, locking out module-only assemblers from ~80% of the market. Integrated players like Emmvee, Waaree, Premier Energies, and Adani Solar enjoy a structural advantage, though the enlisted cell base has widened to ~30 GW across ~10+ manufacturers as of mid-2026 — the moat is real but less exclusive than in 2025.

Revenue drivers: Spread (module selling price minus cell cost), PLI incentives per watt, order book size, and capacity utilisation. Margins expand when raw material costs fall and regulation tightens the competitive field.

📅 ALMM List-II Extension (latest update): On July 18, 2026, MNRE extended the ALMM List-II exemption for net-metering and open-access renewable projects from a May 31, 2026 cut-off to December 31, 2026 — giving these project categories more time to keep sourcing non-ALMM-II cells. MNRE was explicit that this is a phased glide path, not a policy rollback: "no change" to the underlying mandate. From January 1, 2027, even these exempted categories must fully comply with ALMM List-I (modules) and ALMM List-II (cells).

⚠️ Model 2 — Pure Module Assembler (Non-Integrated)
These companies import solar cells (mostly from China) and assemble them into modules. Until 2025, this was a viable model in India — companies like Vikram Solar, Goldi Solar, and many smaller players operated this way. They focus purely on assembly: stringing, lamination, framing, and testing.

Critical vulnerability: ALMM List-II, in force since June 1, 2026, bars module-only assemblers from supplying to ~₹3–4 lakh crore worth of government-mandated solar projects annually. MNRE has since granted a phased carve-out — net-metering and open-access projects retain an exemption until December 31, 2026 — but from January 2027 even these segments must comply, tightening the squeeze further.

Additional squeeze: Anti-Dumping Duty (23–30%) on Chinese cells makes their raw material costlier, while Basic Customs Duty (44% effective on imported modules) doesn't help if they import cells either. Their margin model is under severe stress.
❌ ALMM-II ExcludedNo PLI⚠️ ADD on Imported Cells
🌍 Model 3 — IPP / Project Developer (Pure Downstream)
Independent Power Producers (IPPs) like Adani Green, ReNew, Greenko, and NTPC Renewable do not manufacture panels at all. They design, build, own, and operate solar power plants. They procure modules from manufacturers like Emmvee.

Revenue model: They sell electricity at long-term fixed tariffs (Power Purchase Agreements / PPAs) to DISCOMs or direct consumers (C&I). Revenue is highly predictable; risk comes from tariff discovery, land acquisition, grid connectivity, and financing costs.

Why this matters for Emmvee: India's 500 GW renewable target by 2030 requires 45–50 GW of solar installation annually. IPPs are Emmvee's primary customers, and their mega-pipeline drives Emmvee's 9.9 GW order book.
Stable PPA RevenueEmmvee's Customer Base500 GW Pipeline
🔀 Model 4 — Hybrid (Manufacturer + Developer)
Some players combine manufacturing with power project development. Waaree Energies (India's largest solar manufacturer by capacity at ~12 GW) and Reliance New Energy aim to be fully vertically integrated — from polysilicon/wafers all the way to power plants.

Emmvee's position: Currently a pure manufacturer with no IPP ambitions. This keeps capex focused and avoids balance sheet leverage from power assets. The Devanahalli 6 GW expansion (FY27) deepens the manufacturing model further. Emmvee is also evaluating an ingot/wafer unit to move upstream.

Part 2 — 8 Key Metrics Every Solar Investor Must Track
📐 Metric 1 — Installed GW Capacity
What it means: Total manufacturing capacity for modules and cells (in gigawatts). Reflects scale, competitive position, and ability to bid for large government tenders (which often require minimum GW eligibility).
Emmvee Q4 FY26: Modules: 10.3 GW · Cells: 2.94 GW (both 100% TOPCon)
Watch for: Cell GW < Module GW means company still imports some cells. Emmvee is rapidly closing this gap with the 6 GW Devanahalli project.
⚙️ Metric 2 — Capacity Utilisation Rate
What it means: Actual production as % of installed capacity. Low utilisation (< 60%) signals weak demand or execution issues. High utilisation (> 80%) signals healthy demand and pricing power.
Emmvee Q1 FY27: Cell utilisation: 83% (record) · Module utilisation: 45%
Watch for: Cell utilisation is already ahead of the 85%+ FY27 target; module utilisation has more room to climb as the 9.9 GW order book converts into deliveries.
💸 Metric 3 — Module Spread (₹/Watt)
What it means: The difference between module realisation price and manufacturing cost per watt. This is the core operating margin driver in solar. Spread compresses when module prices fall globally or raw material costs rise.
Emmvee Q4 FY26: EBITDA margin 32.84% — exceptionally high, partly aided by PLI incentives of ~₹0.80–1.00/W
Watch for: PLI phase-out post FY27 may compress reported margins.
📦 Metric 4 — Order Book (GW)
What it means: Contracted but undelivered orders. A large order book provides revenue visibility (typically 6–18 months). Order intake trend (new orders per quarter) indicates demand momentum.
Emmvee Q1 FY27: Order book: 9.9 GW (all-time high, vs 9.4 GW FY26-end) · Q1 FY27 intake: 1.48 GW
Watch for: Order-to-capacity ratio. 9.9 GW vs 10.3 GW module capacity = ~11.5 months cover — healthy.
🏛️ Metric 5 — PLI Incentive (₹/W)
What it means: Production Linked Incentive — a government subsidy of ₹1–2/W paid for 5 years to qualifying manufacturers. Currently inflates EBITDA and PAT margins significantly. Emmvee is a PLI Phase-I winner.
Risk: PLI for Emmvee runs through FY27. Post FY27, margins will normalize downward unless underlying spread improves. Separating PLI-adjusted vs reported margins is critical for valuation.
Watch for: Management disclosures on PLI income each quarter.
🔋 Metric 6 — Operating Cashflow vs PAT
What it means: In manufacturing, PAT must convert to cash. If OCF << PAT, working capital is being consumed (receivables building up, payables shrinking). This is a major red flag — it means accounting profits are not yet real cash.
Emmvee FY26 RED FLAG: OCF ₹200 Cr vs PAT ₹1,081 Cr. WC days jumped from −13.3 to +88.3. Debtor days rose: 38.9 → 50.2.
Watch for: Quarterly normalisation. If OCF doesn't recover in FY27, it signals structural receivables stress.
🔬 Metric 7 — Technology Generation
What it means: Solar cell technology evolves rapidly: BSF (old) → PERC (dominant 2020–24) → TOPCon (current premium) → HJT/Perovskite (future). Manufacturers on older tech face margin compression and order loss.
Emmvee FY26–Q1 FY27: 100% TOPCon transition complete (both cells and modules); record 83% cell utilisation in Q1 FY27.
Watch for: HJT roadmap — Reliance has already entered ALMM List-II with HJT cells, a signal the next disruption cycle is beginning. TOPCon has a 3–5 year window before this becomes a bigger competitive factor.
📊 Metric 8 — Net Debt / Equity
What it means: Solar manufacturing is capital-intensive. High debt increases financial risk, especially if margins compress. A net cash position enables aggressive expansion without dilution.
Emmvee post-IPO (Nov 2025): Net Debt/Equity = (0.06)× — net cash company. ₹1,621 Cr of IPO proceeds used to prepay loans. ICRA upgraded to 'A' in Jan 2026.
Watch for: Devanahalli capex (6 GW) will require ₹3,000–4,000 Cr. Debt will rise in FY27–28.

🏢 Business Description
Emmvee Photovoltaic Power is one of India's leading solar module and cell manufacturers, headquartered in Bengaluru. Founded in 1993, the company has transformed into a fully integrated TOPCon solar manufacturer with 10.3 GW of module capacity and 2.94 GW of cell capacity as of March 2026.

Emmvee went public on NSE in November 2025, raising ₹2,900 Cr — primarily to prepay ₹1,621 Cr in debt, making it a net cash company. It is a PLI Phase-I winner, one of only 6 ALMM List-II approved cell manufacturers, and a key beneficiary of India's solar manufacturing policy regime.

The company sells modules primarily to large IPPs and government projects across India, with an increasingly strong export presence.

🧭 Peer Review Takeaways
Scale: Waaree remains the clear scale leader by a wide margin (5× Emmvee's revenue), with a diversified retail/service/overseas mix. Premier Energies sits between the two on revenue but trades at the richest multiple, reflecting its faster capacity ramp (new 5.6 GW Telangana line, plus BESS and aluminium-frame backward integration announced in July 2026).

Margins & growth: Emmvee posted the fastest FY26 revenue growth (+116% YoY) among the three, and its Q1 FY27 EBITDA margin (35.24%) is now competitive with, or ahead of, peer guidance ranges — though Waaree's FY27 EBITDA guidance of ₹7,000–7,700 Cr dwarfs Emmvee's ₹2,200–2,400 Cr in absolute terms.

Integration: Adani Solar and Waaree are furthest along on backward integration (Adani fully integrated; Waaree adding BESS, electrolyzers, transformers under "Waaree 2.0"). Emmvee's Devanahalli project narrows but does not close this gap, and Emmvee remains the only one of the four still "evaluating" — rather than committing to — ingot/wafer capacity.

Valuation: Emmvee's post-Q1 TTM P/E (~19×) now sits close to Waaree's (~21×) and well below Premier's (~31×) — the earnings beat has meaningfully closed what was a much wider valuation gap at Q4 FY26.

🧮 Reading the Cash Conversion Cycle
Debtor days (50.2): how long Emmvee waits to collect cash after a sale — up from 30 days in FY25, consistent with slower government/DISCOM/IPP payment cycles as the order book scaled.
Inventory days (225): the biggest single driver of the FY26 working-capital build — nearly 7.5 months of inventory on the books, reflecting raw-material stocking (polysilicon, wafers, cells) ahead of the capacity ramp and, per pre-results analyst previews, an inventory stockpile in the ~₹1,700 Cr range heading into Q1 FY27 that management is expected to normalise as production throughput rises.
Days payable (116): Emmvee is also taking longer to pay its own suppliers — up from 91 days in FY25 — which partly offsets the receivables/inventory drag.
Net effect: Cash Conversion Cycle = Debtor Days + Inventory Days − Days Payable = 50.2 + 225 − 116 ≈ 159 days. That's the number of days cash is tied up in the operating cycle before it comes back in — and it's the single best explanation for why FY26 OCF (₹200 Cr) lagged PAT (₹1,081 Cr) so sharply. CB Research will watch whether Q1–Q4 FY27 filings show inventory days normalising as Devanahalli ramps production, since that is the largest lever available to shorten the cycle.
⚠️ Working Capital Red Flag — Deep Dive (Updated Q1 FY27)
FY26 OCF of ₹200.14 Cr vs PAT of ₹1,081.55 Cr represented only 18.5% cash conversion. Working capital days had swung from (13.3) to +88.3 (screener still flags this as of the Q1 update), and debtor days sat at 50.2 as of FY26-end. This reflects large receivables from government/DISCOM and IPP clients — a common feature of the Indian solar supply chain.

Risk: Q1 FY27's sequential revenue and PAT dip (-10.5%/-3.1% QoQ) came off a seasonally strong Q4; if working capital continues to build as Devanahalli capex ramps through FY27, the net-cash cushion could erode faster than modelled.
Mitigant: Devanahalli's ₹5,500 Cr capex is already funded (₹3,300 Cr tied-up debt at <8%), so near-term OCF stress need not force fresh unplanned borrowing. Record Q1 margins (35.24% EBITDA) also suggest underlying profitability is genuine, not solely PLI-driven. CB Research will track OCF/PAT conversion each quarter through FY27 as the key data point to resolve this flag.

🏰 Competitive Moats Structural Advantages · Durability Assessment
🏆 Regulatory Moat (Widening, Still High Conviction)
ALMM List-II — Now Live, But a Growing Club (10+ Players, ~30.5 GW)
ALMM List-II has been in force since June 1, 2026: only enlisted cell manufacturers can supply modules to government-funded solar projects (~80% of total Indian solar demand), instantly excluding ~1,100+ smaller module-only assemblers. However, the enlisted base has grown quickly — from 6 players/13 GW at the August 2025 launch to 10+ players/~30.5 GW by mid-2026, with Reliance's HJT cells the latest entrant. The moat still excludes pure assemblers, but it is no longer a scarce 6-player club — competitive intensity among the enlisted integrated players (Waaree, Adani, Premier, Emmvee, and others) is the more relevant lens going forward. MNRE's July 2026 clarifications also softened near-term enforcement for net-metering/open-access projects (exempt till Dec 2026), a modest dilution of the near-term demand pull-through.
Durability: HIGH vs assemblers, MEDIUM vs integrated peers Type: Regulatory Barrier
⚡ Technology Moat
100% TOPCon Transition — First-Mover in Indian Integrated Manufacturing
Emmvee completed 100% TOPCon conversion for both cells and modules in FY26 — among the fastest in India. TOPCon cells achieve ~24–25% efficiency vs ~21–22% for PERC (older technology). Higher efficiency means fewer modules per MW, lower balance-of-system costs, and premium realisations for customers. Record 83% cell utilisation in Q1 FY27 shows this capacity is now being used efficiently, not just built.
Durability: MEDIUM (3-5 yr window before HJT) Type: Tech Leadership
📦 Scale & Execution Moat
10.3 GW Module Capacity — Top-5 Indian Manufacturer
Scale in solar manufacturing creates cost advantages through bulk procurement of encapsulants, glass, backsheet, junction boxes, and aluminium frames. Emmvee's 10.3 GW positions it in the top tier of Indian manufacturers. Large IPPs and government agencies strongly prefer suppliers who can deliver GW-scale volumes in short timelines. Emmvee's order book hit an all-time high of 9.9 GW in Q1 FY27, with the repeat-customer share rising to 57% — a direct signal of delivery-execution trust.
Durability: MEDIUM (Waaree larger at 12 GW) Type: Scale Advantage
🛡️ Policy Umbrella Moat
BCD + ADD = 44%+ Protection from Chinese Imports
The combination of 44% effective Basic Customs Duty on imported modules and 23–30% Anti-Dumping Duty on Chinese cells/modules (valid till Sep 2028) creates a near-impenetrable price wall. Chinese module prices (which fell below ₹12–14/W globally) are effectively priced out of the Indian market. This sustains Emmvee's domestic realisations well above global market prices.
Durability: MEDIUM (policy-dependent; BCD subject to review) Type: Policy Protection
🌿 Brand & Relationship Moat
30+ Years of Solar Manufacturing — Institutional Customer Trust
Founded in 1993, Emmvee has 30+ years of solar manufacturing experience — rare in an industry where most Indian players are under 10 years old. Long-standing relationships with IPPs (Adani Green, ReNew, NTPC Renewable etc.) and state DISCOMs create switching cost advantages. These customers prefer suppliers with proven quality, delivery reliability, and service history.
Durability: HIGH (relationships are sticky) Type: Customer Relationships
⚠️ Moat Vulnerabilities
Where Emmvee's Moats Are Weak
1. No proprietary technology: TOPCon is a licensed/open technology. Competitors can replicate.
2. No wafer/ingot moat: Still dependent on imports for upstream silicon products.
3. No pricing power beyond policy umbrella: Module prices are commoditised globally.
4. ALMM moat is government-made, not market-made: Policy change risk is real.
5. Waaree at 12 GW and Adani (fully integrated) are ahead in scale and integration respectively.
Risk: Policy Reversal Risk: HJT Disruption

☀️ Emmvee Solar — CB Research Summary Verdict (Q1 FY27)
Emmvee Photovoltaic Power opened FY27 with its strongest-ever first quarter — revenue up 51% YoY to ₹1,555.5 Cr and PAT more than doubling (+103% YoY) to ₹380.3 Cr, with record EBITDA (35.24%) and PAT (24.2%) margins driven by record cell utilisation (83%) and deeper internal cell consumption. Two threads from FY26 still need resolving. First, the PLI cliff — PLI Phase-I ends FY27, and while Q1 margins suggest the underlying business is strengthening independent of the subsidy, the true post-PLI run-rate won't be visible until FY28. Second, the working capital divergence — FY26 OCF of just ₹200 Cr against PAT of ₹1,081 Cr (debtor days 50.2, WC days 88.3) has not yet been demonstrably reversed; this bears close tracking through FY27 as Devanahalli capex draws down cash. A third, newer thread has emerged: the ALMM List-II moat, while still a real barrier against pure assemblers, has widened from 6 to 10+ enlisted manufacturers since its 2025 launch — durability now depends more on execution and integration depth than on regulatory scarcity.

At ~19× TTM P/E, Emmvee now trades close to Waaree and at a meaningful discount to Premier Energies — the Q1 beat has closed much of the earlier valuation gap. The Devanahalli module line (Dec 2026) and cell line (Mar 2027) are the next critical execution checkpoints.
