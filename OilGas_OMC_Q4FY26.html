Indian Oil Corporation Ltd — NSE: IOC · Founded 1959 · CMD: Arvinder Singh Sahney · India's Largest OMC · Maharatna
Business Overview & Q4 FY26 Results

Indian Oil Corporation (IOCL) — India's largest oil company by revenues and Asia's #1 refiner. Operates 11 refineries (~80+ MLPA capacity) including the world's largest single-location refinery at Panipat. Covers the full hydrocarbon value chain: refining, pipelines, marketing, petrochemicals, R&D and gas.

Q4 FY26 Highlights: Consolidated PAT surged 81% YoY to ₹15,176 Cr driven by strong GRM estimated at $16–19/bbl. Revenue grew 7% YoY to ₹2,36,899 Cr. Concern: Petrochemicals segment posted EBIT losses in Q3 (₹36.2 Bn) amid weak margins. Capex target FY26: ₹347 Bn. Domestic sales volume grew 14.5%/4.8% QoQ/YoY in Q3. Stock lost 18.7% YTD in CY2026.

GAIL (India) Limited — NSE: GAIL · CMD: Sandeep Kumar Gupta · 70% Gas Transmission Market Share · Maharatna
Business Overview & Q4 FY26 Results

GAIL (India) — India's dominant natural gas company. Commands ~70% market share in gas transmission and supplies >50% of all natural gas sold in India. Business spans gas marketing, transmission, petrochemicals, LPG, and City Gas Distribution (CGD).

Q4 FY26 — Hit Hard by Middle East LNG Disruption: Standalone PAT collapsed 38.4% to ₹1,262 Cr. Revenue fell 2.5%. Root cause: West Asia conflict disrupted Qatar and Gulf LNG shipping routes; industrial customers (power plants, fertilisers) cut gas consumption ~5% YoY. Petrochemicals segment revenue fell 15.4%. Only gas transmission (+11.6%) remained resilient.

Silver linings: GAIL commissioned ~2,000 km new pipeline in FY26 including Mumbai-Nagpur-Jharsuguda. Gas marketing volume FY26: 104.21 MMSCMD (up from 101.49). Dividend payout ratio of 52% — highest ever. Interim dividend ₹5/share already paid.

Petronet LNG Limited — NSE: PETRONET · CMD: A K Singh · JV: GAIL+IOCL+BPCL+ONGC · Dahej & Kochi Terminals
Business Overview & Q4 FY26 Results

Petronet LNG — India's largest LNG regasification company. Operates Dahej terminal (17.5 MMTPA; India's biggest LNG terminal) and Kochi terminal (5 MMTPA). A JV with four Maharatna PSUs as equal sponsors. Provides regasification services — does not trade LNG.

Q4 FY26 — The Paradox: Revenue fell 23.3% YoY to ₹9,442 Cr yet PAT jumped 25% to ₹1,338 Cr. Why? LNG spot prices fell globally → revenue formula-linked to LNG prices fell. But Petronet's contracted buy-price from Qatar (Rasgas) is long-term fixed, so cost fell faster (−29.1%). Margin expanded. Additionally, Use-or-Pay (UoP) provision reversals of prior quarters boosted Q4 PAT. LNG volume 219 TBTU; Dahej utilisation 90.1%; Kochi hit record annual throughput 68 TBTU.

Analyst consensus: JPMorgan Overweight TP ₹335; Investec initiated Buy TP ₹400.

🛢️ GRM — The Single Most Important Metric for Indian Refiners
For every Indian refiner — IOCL, HPCL, BPCL, CPCL, MRPL — the Gross Refining Margin (GRM) is the single number that determines whether a quarter is good or bad. It can swing profitability by thousands of crores in one quarter. A refiner with flat volumes but surging GRM (like HPCL in Q4 FY26: $14.27/bbl) posts record profits. A refiner with growing volumes but collapsing GRM posts terrible results. Understanding GRM — its formula, drivers, and comparison to the Singapore benchmark — is essential to reading any OMC result.
What Is GRM? — Definition, Formula & Real Impact Example

GRM = Revenue from all refined products sold − Cost of crude oil purchased, in US$/barrel ($/bbl) of crude processed.

A refinery buys crude at e.g. $105/bbl, cracks it into petrol, diesel, ATF, naphtha, LPG, bitumen, fuel oil. Each product fetches a different market price. Sum of product revenues minus crude cost = GRM.

GRM Formula
Product Revenue / bbl
− Crude Oil Cost / bbl
− Variable Opex / bbl
= GRM ($/bbl)

🔑 Why Indian GRMs Consistently Beat Singapore

Since March 2022, India buys Russian Urals crude at $8–15/bbl below Brent. Singapore refineries price against Dubai (Middle East) crude. Indian refiners using discounted Russian crude therefore hold a structural cost advantage of $3–8/bbl over Singapore GRM. This "Russia premium" was the hidden engine of India's OMC profit boom in FY24 and FY26. Risk: if Russia crude discount narrows (sanctions eased / routing issues), India's outperformance shrinks.

Crack Spreads by Product — How Each Product Drives Overall GRM

A crack spread = price of a refined product − price of crude. It measures how much a refinery earns on that product. A refinery's GRM = weighted average of all crack spreads × respective product yield fractions. Refineries maximising ATF + Diesel (high distillate yield) have structurally better GRMs.

GMM is specific to Indian OMCs. It measures the profitability of the fuel retail business — independent of refining. It is the gap between the retail selling price of petrol/diesel and the cost to acquire or produce it, after distribution costs.

GRM vs GMM — The Two Pillars of OMC Profitability

Indian OMCs (IOCL, BPCL, HPCL) are integrated — they refine crude (GRM) AND retail fuel (GMM). Total profitability = GRM + GMM + Petrochemicals + Gas. Pure refiners like CPCL have only GRM — no retail exposure — giving cleaner results. The key tension: GRM and GMM often move in opposite directions when crude spikes — exactly as in Q4 FY26 when GRM boomed but GMM went negative.

How Under-Recovery Works — Explained

What is under-recovery? The government mandates a retail price for domestic LPG cylinders. This price is politically sensitive and rarely raised. When global LNG/crude prices rise, the actual cost to OMCs exceeds the mandated sale price. The difference = under-recovery.

Retail Price History and Policy Decisions — Petrol & Diesel

FY22 (Russia-Ukraine): Crude hit $120/bbl. Government froze prices for 137 days through UP elections, then allowed hikes of ₹10/litre petrol and ₹7/litre diesel in March-April 2022. OMCs absorbed the pain, then recovered over 2 quarters as crude fell back.

FY25 (partial relief): Crude fell to $70-80. OMCs actually cut prices ₹2/litre in March 2024 before elections. Marketing margins improved significantly.

Q4 FY26 / FY27 (current): Crude surged to ~$120/bbl (West Asia conflict); settled ~$105/bbl. Government allowed ₹4/litre hike in petrol/diesel — insufficient. OMCs losing ~₹500 Cr/day combined. The question for FY27: will government allow further hikes before more state elections?

Inventory Gains/Losses — How They Impact OMC Profitability

Inventory Effect is the change in value of crude oil and product stocks held at a refinery due to price movements. It is a major but often-overlooked driver of OMC quarterly profits.

Crude Price MovementInventory ImpactOn Reported GRMOn PAT
Rising crude (Q4 FY26: $72→$105)Inventory GAIN — old cheap stock sold at high priceReported GRM > Core GRMBoosts PAT by ₹1,000–3,500 Cr/quarter per OMC
Falling crude (FY25 H2)Inventory LOSS — expensive stock sold at lower priceReported GRM < Core GRMDrags PAT by ₹500–2,000 Cr/quarter
Stable crudeMinimal inventory effectReported ≈ Core GRMPAT reflects true operating performance

Why analysts use Core GRM: To strip out the inventory distortion and see the true refining economics. IOCL in Q3 FY26 had reported GRM of $12.22/bbl but core GRM of $13.50/bbl — the core is the more reliable profitability signal. In Q4 FY26, rising crude added an estimated ₹3,200 Cr inventory gain for IOCL, ₹2,100 Cr for BPCL, and ₹1,800 Cr for HPCL.

FY27 Outlook · Scenario Analysis · Company-Wise View · CB Research
⚡ FY26 Was the Banner Year — FY27 Is the Test

What Made FY26 Exceptional: Combined OMC PAT crossed ₹77,281 Cr — more than double FY25. HPCL's standalone PAT grew 133%, IOCL's nearly tripled. BPCL delivered best-ever annual profitability. The reasons: declining crude in H1 FY26 (to $72/bbl), high GRMs from West Asia conflict in Q4, and Russia crude discount throughout.

Why FY27 Is At Risk: The same West Asia conflict that boosted Q4 GRMs has now locked crude at $105/bbl — and government retail price control means OMCs cannot pass this through. GAIL's LNG supply remains disrupted. The structural advantage of cheap H1 FY26 crude no longer exists. The question is not whether FY27 will be lower than FY26 — it will. The question is how much lower.
